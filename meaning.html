The Meaning of Life

July 2026

When people reflect on the meaning of life, they often approach the question as they would any other practical question: What is something for? A chair exists to provide a place to sit. A knife is made to cut. We naturally assume that everything must have a purpose. It is therefore easy to extend the same way of thinking to ourselves and ask, "What is the purpose of my life?"

But perhaps this is the wrong question. More often than not, what we really want to know is whether our life is useful. We wonder whether we contribute enough, whether we matter to others, or whether we have accomplished something significant. In other words, we try to measure the value of our existence by what we do rather than by the simple fact that we are.

Modern society encourages this way of thinking. We are taught to value productivity, achievement, and success. It becomes easy to believe that our place in the world must be earned through effort, accomplishment, or service. Without realizing it, we begin to evaluate ourselves in the same way we evaluate tools: by their usefulness.

Yet beneath this search for purpose often lies something much deeper—a quiet uncertainty about our own existence. Many people do not fully experience their existence as something natural and unquestionable. Instead, they feel they must justify it. They strive to become successful, helpful, or indispensable because they hope that doing so will finally allow them to feel worthy of being here.

This search can become exhausting. If our right to exist depends on what we achieve, then there is always another goal to reach, another expectation to fulfil, another standard to meet. No accomplishment can provide lasting security because the need to prove ourselves never truly ends.

There is another way of living. Imagine recognizing that your existence requires no justification. Before any role, identity, achievement, or reputation, there is simply the fact that you exist. Nothing needs to be added to make that reality complete. Existence itself is enough.

When we live from this understanding, our relationship with others changes. Giving is no longer an obligation or a way of earning love and acceptance. Instead, generosity arises naturally. We share because we genuinely want to, not because we need something in return. Love expresses itself effortlessly when it is no longer tied to the need for approval.

When we experience an inner sense of abundance, giving becomes a source of joy rather than exhaustion. Gratitude naturally overflows into kindness. There is no pressure, only the freedom to contribute because it feels like the most natural expression of being alive.

The medieval mystic Meister Eckhart captured this beautifully when he wrote, "I live in order to live, without asking why." These words remind us that life does not need to justify itself. It simply is.

Perhaps the meaning of life is not found in becoming someone special or accomplishing something extraordinary. Perhaps it is found in being fully present. When we are completely here, attentive to this moment, allowing ourselves, others, and life itself to be exactly as they are, we discover something remarkable.

Presence naturally gives rise to love. And love is not something we manufacture through effort. It appears when we stop resisting life and begin meeting it with openness. In that openness there is a deep sense of belonging, a quiet peace, and a wholehearted "yes" to life.

Maybe that is the meaning we have been searching for all along—not something we must achieve, but something we can experience whenever we are fully present to the miracle of simply being alive.

This article was inspired by the ideas explored in Christian Meyer's Ein Kurs in wahrem Loslassen: Durch das Tor des Fühlens zu innerer Freiheit (Arkana, 2015).
